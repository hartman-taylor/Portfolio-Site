Electro-Mechanical Design

In order to demostrate linear movement in a machine with 2.5 degrees of freedom, I aimed to make a machine that could stack blocks. Initially, I was worried about having to design too many parts, but after thinking it through, I realized if designed properly, I only needed to design 4 parts; I could then use the same parts in multiple different places. I was supplied with aliminium rails and stepper motors, so I designed the parts around the dimensions of those components. All red parts (brackets, carriages, carts) were designed in SolidWorks and 3D printed; Explination of each individual part is on the next page. The set of motors mounted on elevated platforms made from 2x4's, control the movement in the horizontal direction; I completed all of the parts for horizontal movement first, to make sure everything worked as planned. I then moved to the vertical axes. The carriages on the horizontal axes attatch to carts with bearings on the bottom in order to smoothly support the vertical axes. Additionally, these carts house the motors used for movement along the vertical axes. All motion is acheived with stepper driven belts mounted to alimunum rails. The blocks are picked up with an electromagnet fashioned out of a bolt wrapped in copper wire; this requires around 3.5 Amps of current from an external power source, which is toggle on and off via relay. This toggling along with all other motor control is done with a microcontroller, commanded with GCode. The final product worked as expected (demonstrated in video to the left). It successfully had 2.5 degrees of freedom, the first two being movement in the horizontal and vertical directions; The third was the toggling of the electromagnet.

Cart houses the motors for the vertical axes, along with providing support for the vertical alimunum bars, and mounting points for the bearings which allow for the entire aparatus to slide along the horizontal axes. The idea here was to reduce friction with the ground as much as possible, while holding all the necissary components. The four screw holes secure the motor to the part. Two bearings were mounted on the bottom, using the two holes shown along with a third bearing and hole, which are obstructed in the current view. Althought the wheels should have been built with a bit more support, the part functioned as expected.

Bar Mount is designed to hold the horizontal axes off of the ground, as well as keeping them stable and offering a mounting point for the belt bearings. I added the notch in the inside to further support the bar from sliding withing the square, which had to be designed with a fairly large tolerance due to the uncertainty and inaccuracy of the 3D printed supports. This piece successfully held the axes in place securely, while still having enough tolerance to be slipped on and off when needed.

Motor Mount aims to house the motors for the horizontal axis. The top screw holes secure the motor to the part and the bottom screw holes secure the part to the ground. I initially thought that I should enclose all 4 sides; but after beginning the design, I realized it would be easier for wire management and easy of assembly to leave one side open. The part worked as intended and was stong enough to keep the motor from moving undesirably.

Carriage is intended to move along the aluminum bars using the grove at the bottom. The ridged indent is used to attatch to the belt and the open groove allows the belt to pass through in the other direction unobstructed. The sqaure hole to the right is fitted to hold one of the aluminium rails; This hole ended up fitting very well, as it was loose enough to slide around when force was applied, but not so loose that the rails jiggled around.

Rapid Prototyping

Casting and Molding

In order to practice casting and molding, I was tasked with making a stamp. I designed the mold for the stamp in SolidWorks then 3D printed it and cast a silicon model with it. After testing the silicon stamp, I designed and 3D printed a two piece enclosure for the silicon. Finally, I attatched the two pieces with glue. The final stamp worked well, as can be seen in the pictures above.

Heat Tool Design

A 20 gauge nichrome wire heats up enough to cut foam when 4.3 Amps of current is passed through it. My task was to utilize this fact to make a heat tool which can carve things out of foam. However the wire becomes much too hot to hold and if the ends are touched together it short circuits. To solve these problems I made a heat proof enclosure with an on/off switch. I thought 2 pieces would be ideal, for easy of assembly and repair. The idea of the switch was intended to make handling easier without worry of being shocked while messing with the leads. First I designed a two piece enclosure in SolidWorks. The two pieces attatch with pins and include holes for the wires and switch; this means they can be easily dissassembled, in order to maximize ease of repair and disassembly. I then designed a custom mold to fit in the designated space in the enclosure. After 3D printing the enclosure and mold, I created the main nichrome wire piece by bending 6 inches of nichrome wire into the correct shape and attatching copper wire leads to the ends. I then cast around it in the mold with epoxy. The mold itself is designed to be part of the final product rather than being peeled off. Finally I put all of the components together and soldered the wired connections. It successfully cut through foam after testing. The only part of design I would change if I did it again would be to have supports on both sides of the nichrome loop; this way it would not move around as much when cutting.

Mechanisms

Trash Can Mechanism

In order to practice mechanism design and create a trashcan that is easily usable from any angle, I aimed to design a trash can with a tray that transfered trash from the top into the can. I had to make sure that the tray would not flip over during the movement process; Additionally I had to make sure the tray did not collide with the can itself and that the connections were all in the body of the trash can. Initially I did not know how to go about this, but I soon realized I just needed to synthesisze a four bar mechanism that reached three key positions. The fourth bar is modeled not as a bar itself but as the distance between the two mounting points on the trash can. In class, I made an initial concept drawing in MathIllustrations, which can be seen below. This functioned relatively well, but some of the lengths needed minor adjustments. After making these adjustments, I began to build the pieces in SolidWorks. To the right is three pictures of the tray in the three key positions I used to contsruct the four bar mechanism. On the far right is the final product with the path of the tray traced out in Motion Study. As can be seen, the product acheived the task sucessfully. Tweaking the lengths ended up being the most time consuming part, as I had a bit of trouble transfering them precisesly from MathIllustrations to SolidWorks.
